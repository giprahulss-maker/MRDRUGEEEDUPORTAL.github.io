1. Introduction to Human Anatomy and Physiology

Human Anatomy and Physiology together form the scientific foundation of all medical, pharmaceutical, nursing, and allied health sciences. A clear understanding of the structure of the human body and the mechanisms by which it functions is essential before studying pathology, pharmacology, or therapeutics.

Anatomy answers: ‚ÄúWhat is the structure?‚Äù
Physiology answers: ‚ÄúHow does it function?‚Äù

These two disciplines are inseparable. The structure of a body part determines the way it functions ‚Äî a concept known as the principle of complementarity of structure and function. When structure changes due to injury or disease, function is often impaired.

For example, the alveoli of the lungs are thin-walled and highly vascular to facilitate rapid gas exchange. If the alveolar walls thicken (as in pulmonary fibrosis), oxygen diffusion becomes impaired, demonstrating how structural alteration affects physiological performance.

2. Definition and Branches of Anatomy

Anatomy is the scientific study of body structures and the relationships among them. It includes both macroscopic (gross) and microscopic examination of the human body.

Major Branches of Anatomy

Branch Description Clinical Relevance
Gross Anatomy Study of structures visible to the naked eye Essential for surgery and physical examination
Regional Anatomy Study of body by specific regions Important for surgical planning
Systemic Anatomy Study of organ systems Understanding integrated body functions
Microscopic Anatomy Study of cells (cytology) and tissues (histology) Basis of pathology and biopsy interpretation
Developmental Anatomy Study of structural changes from fertilization to adulthood Understanding congenital anomalies
Surface & Clinical Anatomy Study of anatomical landmarks and applied anatomy Bedside examination, radiology

Modern anatomy integrates cadaveric dissection, imaging techniques (X-ray, CT, MRI), and digital visualization tools to correlate structure with clinical practice.

3. Definition and Subdivisions of Physiology

Physiology is the scientific study of normal body functions and regulatory mechanisms that maintain life. It explains how cells, tissues, organs, and systems coordinate to sustain homeostasis.

Major Subdivisions

  • Cell Physiology ‚Äì Transport mechanisms, membrane potentials, enzyme activity
  • Systemic Physiology ‚Äì Cardiac, renal, respiratory physiology
  • Neurophysiology ‚Äì Nerve impulse transmission, reflex arcs
  • Endocrine Physiology ‚Äì Hormonal regulation
  • Pathophysiology ‚Äì Functional disturbances during disease
  • Exercise Physiology ‚Äì Adaptations during physical activity

Understanding physiology is crucial in pharmacology, as most drugs act by modifying physiological processes such as enzyme activity, receptor binding, or ion channel function.

4. Levels of Structural Organization

The human body is organized in a hierarchical manner. Each level builds upon the one below it to create increasing structural and functional complexity.

Level Description Example
Chemical Atoms and molecules forming biological compounds Water, Glucose, DNA
Cellular Basic structural and functional unit of life RBC, Neuron
Tissue Group of similar cells performing a common function Muscle tissue
Organ Two or more tissues forming a functional structure Heart, Liver
Organ System Group of organs working together Digestive system
Organism Complete living individual Human being

Disruption at any level can influence higher levels. For example, cellular injury can impair tissue function, which may ultimately affect an entire organ system.

7. Homeostasis

Homeostasis refers to the maintenance of a relatively stable internal environment despite continuous external changes. It is achieved primarily through feedback mechanisms.

Negative Feedback

Reverses deviations from a set point. Example: regulation of body temperature and blood glucose.

Positive Feedback

Amplifies a process until a specific outcome is achieved. Example: childbirth and blood clotting.

Failure of homeostasis may result in pathological conditions such as diabetes mellitus, hypertension, acidosis, or shock.
